Web Writing 101

Writing for the web can pose new challenges even for experienced writers.

Web writing can be a discipline unto itself, but there are some basics that every reader should keep in mind when creating content for the web:

Concision

People scan and browse content much more rapidly on the web than they do in printed materials. If users can't answer "What's in it for me?" within the first couple minutes on a page, they are likely to lose interest and search for content elsewhere.

When auditing exisiting content or creating new pages for AAP.org, worry less about formalities and more about getting your end-user what he/she wants. Be concise and clear, brief but comprehensive.

Pare down choices for users. The days of putting everything on a web page are long gone. It's better to provide your user with a narrative through content than to give them hundreds of options that eventuate in decision fatigue. With AAP.org's current global navigation, end-users are provided with close to 200 options on any given page within the AAP.org site collection. This muddies our message and intentions. The content and the site are about marketing the AAP brand.

Outdated Methods

Avoid use of the following when creating content for your website:

  1. Red text to show emphasis or elicit a user's attention. Outside of a few usability tests in China, red text has been shown to evoke hostility in end-users.
  2. Blinking text. Blinking text was popular in the early days of the web. However, usability testing proved that blinking text distracted and perturbed end-users. Some companies (e.g., IBM) forbid use of blinking elements as part of their accessibility policy because flashing text can cause photosensitive epileptic seizures in susceptible individuals.
  3. "Click here." This was once the most commonly used call to action on the web; however, AAP.org is now responsive and takes a mobile-first approach to user interactions. You cannot "click" on a smartphone or tablet.
  4. "Welcome to..." It may seem polite to welcome users to each individual section of your site, but introductory text takes up valuable real estate on your page. It is more common for an end-user of AAP.org to browse than to search. This means that a user bouncing between subsites on AAP.org doesn't need to be welcomed to each section of content. Remember that your end-users are more interested in what you can do them for them than whether you're polite.

Calls to Action

  1. Write short verb phrases
  2. Calls should be written in the first person (e.g., "Show My Subscriptions" is better than "Show Your Subscriptions")
  3. Don't use "Click here."
  4. If you find your calls to action are explanations for otherwise obvious UX standards (e.g., you write two paragraphs explaining to users how to enter terms into a search field), it's better to fix the structure of the page/site than to add unnecessary text to a page. User experience is rooted in expectation, and vice versa.

Heading Structure

Follow a consistent heading structure. A heading structure is not the same as the visual styles of your headings, which are determined by the site's cascading stylesheets (CSS).

Keep your top-level headings (page titles) short but informative. Don't title articles in reference to other chunks of content on the site, since your web page may be the point at which users enter the AAP.org site collection. "Introduction" may seem sufficient when contained in the beginning of a book, but if it's the first page a user sees in a website, it doesn't provide enough information to help your user discern context.

PDFs

Usability studies have consistently shown that PDFs are not ideal for web content. The following are just a few examples of why you should think twice about posting PDFs in instances where providing web pages incurs no additional resources:

PDFs...

  • Do not respond to screen size, thus negating the benefits of responsive design.
  • Lose all site navigation, making it more difficult for users to move around the site collection.
  • Do not comply with Section 508 (accessibility), meaning that they are more difficult to access for users with disabilities.
  • Are more difficult to read within the browser.
  • Preclude valuable user data. Google Analytics will only tell you if a PDF link has been selected, not whether the document has been saved locally for future reference. Google analytics is unable to measure how long a user stays on a pdf "page" because the content needs to be saved locally (even if it's just in a browser's cache) before being viewed..
  • Are perceived as "unprofessional" or "lazy" in usability tests.
  • Break the mental mode of how the Internet is supposed to work (ie, directories with subdirectories of web pages).
  • Are usually much larger than web pages and therefore require more bandwidth and storage for end-users.
  • Are not cached. In other words, your web browser saves some of the files needed to load a web page, which means faster page loads and better SEO (search engine optimization). The resources needed for a PDF are not cacheable..
  • Usually do not contain internal navigation, which only compounds the problem of losing the global navigation from the website.
  • Open according to different behaviors depending on the end-user's browser settings and default PDF reader. Some browsers may automatically open a PDF in a new tab, whereas others download the file to a default location in the file system.

Content that is very large (e.g., a 300-page book) or very well established as a standalone product may benefit from remaining in a fixed/print format.

Grammar and Usage

You can sometimes break conventional (ie, print-based) usage and grammatical rules in web writing. A good example is the spell-out-all-numbers-one-through-ten rule, which can be overlooked in cases where numerics promote concision without hindering comprehension. This applies even more so to articles that revolve around tabular data, research results, etc.
