1. Content Types

What is a content type?

A content type is a reusable collection of metadata for a category of items (ie, contained instances of structured content).

NOTE: Content type is not the same as filetype. The latter is based on filename extension.

Creating a Content Type

Let's look at one line from the first example from the quiz.


                    name_first: Ryan

name_first is often called a key, property, name, field, or attribute.

Ryan is a value or term.

Both of these key-values (property-terms) can be considered metadata about the driver; ie, me.

2. Relationships

Let's look at one of the keys from our staff content type again.


            immediate_supervisor: Monique Terrell

We might notice that this value shows up in multiple instances of the staff content type.

Monique has multiple direct reports.

In this case, the immediate_supervisor key represents a one-to-many relationship in our CMS. This means that the value for immediate_supervisor is then linked to another staff content type, who is linked to other staff, which content type inherits from person.

3.Metadata and Schema

So far, our person-based content types are fine, but what about other content types that require more key-value pairs?

Let's look at an article content type.

article Content Type


    title: Long-Form Article for Cap.org
    date: 2016-03-23
    authors: [Ryan Watters, Nathan Jones]
    categories: [publishing]
    keywords: [web publishing, html, content-first]
    description: This is a description of the article...
        ...whole bunch of other stuff

title and description will be free-form by default, but what about categories and keywords?

Eg What if our editor or writer creates another article and attributes "markup" instead of "html" as a keyword and puts "Ryan Waters" as the first author in the CMS?

Now we have two related pieces of content with no defined relationship.

But why is this a problem?

Computers are dumb.

They need to be told exactly what to do...

...including how to manage inconsistencies.

So how do we control the values being attributed to blocks of structured content?

Enter Controlled Vocabularies*

* Note: You may often hear the word taxonomy which may be correct depending on context, but taxonomy is a more general reference to the process of classification.

Controlled vocabularies allow us to define a well-delimited range of acceptable values.

Well-delimited does not mean large.

A controlled vocabulary could be a handufl of words; eg—


                    toppings:
                        - lettuce
                        - tomato
                        - onion
                        - pickle
                        - relish
                        - celery salt

But sometimes your content is more complex. Robust systems of classifications allow for both synonyms and nesting.

What is nested metadata?

Think of the following in an article:

audience: doctor


                        doctor
                            > pathologist
                            > pediatrician
                                > pediatric_nephrologist
                                > pediatric_hematologist
                            > neurologist

So how do you come up with your own controlled vocabulary?

Luckily, you don't have to. Instead, you can rely on the hard, open-source work of tens of thousands of people.

Enter Metadata Standards

Metadata standards are collections of content rules (how content is formulated), representational rules (eg, capitalization), and allowed element values (eg, our controlled vocabulary).

Here are some examples...

  • DCMI (content, Google)
  • IPTC (photography,media topics)
  • RDF, schema.org (web)

Okay, so what does all this mean?

You want to publish a medical journal.

But few doctors agree on the exact same terms for diseases or medical practices.

This is why MeSH (a controlled vocabulary from the NIH) was developed. With over 125,000 headings and several hierarchical levels, MeSH allows computers to understand what is readily apparent to human physicians.

It establishs a common understanding (ie, the semantics) of a value in our key-value relationships.

Semantics?

Semantics is the study of how a word means.*

For example, a computer doesn't know what a concussion is, but we can teach a computer that a concussion is related to traumatic brain injury, which is related to chronic traumatic encephalitis, etc.

* Note: The study of what a word means is etymology

4. Templating

Why use templates?

  • Consistency for users.
  • Computers are faster at repetition than we are
  • Improve SEO
  • Keep yourself from going insane.

Let's take another look at our article

                title_seo: Better SEO title

Benefits

Ultimately, content modeling benefits the end-user, but if done well, it can improve every facet of your website.

Search

Both external SEO and site search.

We already went over external SEO, but what about facets in your site search? Couldn't a key like discipline be part of a content type?

Management

Having structure metadata, content models, and relationships in your CMS will allow you to make changes once and have them dispersed everywhere.

Customized Content

Without content models and metadata, you can't provide a pathologist in Arizona information specific to their location and practice.

Exportability

Unlike with humans, a high level of structure means a high level of freedom for computers.

Structured data is always easier to export to other systems.
